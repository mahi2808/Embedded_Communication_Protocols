15. Interview Questions:-
1. What is I2C?
I2C is a synchronous serial communication protocol that uses SDA and SCL lines to communicate between a master and multiple slave devices.

2. How many wires are used in I2C?
Two wires:
SDA
SCL

3. Who generates SCL?
Master device generates SCL.

4. Why does each slave need an address?
To identify the target device on a shared bus.

5. What is ACK?
ACK is an acknowledge signal generated by the receiver by pulling SDA LOW.

6. Who generate ACK?
In I²C, the ACK bit is always generated by the receiver. During a write operation, the slave acknowledges both the address and data bytes. During a read operation, the slave acknowledges the address, while the master acknowledges the received data bytes.

7. What is an open-drain output?
An open-drain output can only pull a signal line LOW or release it to a high-impedance state. It cannot drive the line HIGH directly.

8. Why does I²C use open-drain outputs?
I²C uses open-drain outputs because multiple devices share the same SDA and SCL lines. Open-drain prevents bus contention by allowing devices to pull the line LOW but never drive it HIGH, enabling safe multi-device communication.

9. Q. Where is the open-drain transistor located in I²C?
The open-drain transistor is located inside the I²C hardware of each device (microcontroller, sensor, EEPROM, RTC, etc.). The transistor can pull SDA/SCL LOW by connecting the line to GND, or it can be turned OFF to release the line into a high-impedance state.

10. Why are pull-up resistors required in I²C?
I²C devices use open-drain outputs and cannot drive the line HIGH. Pull-up resistors are required to generate the HIGH level when all devices release the bus.

11. What happens if pull-up resistors are removed?
SDA and SCL will float when released. The bus cannot reliably reach a valid HIGH level, causing communication failure.

12. Why is 4.7kΩ commonly used?
It provides a good compromise between rise time and current consumption for standard I²C speeds.

13. Who makes SCL HIGH and LOW in I²C?
The master controls the clock. To generate LOW, the master pulls SCL to GND using its open-drain transistor. To generate HIGH, the master releases SCL, and the pull-up resistor pulls the line to VCC. Therefore, the master controls the clock timing, while the pull-up resistor creates the HIGH level.

14. What is Clock Stretching?
Clock Stretching is a mechanism that allows a slave device to temporarily pause communication by holding the SCL line LOW.

15. What is Clock Stretching?
Clock Stretching is an I²C feature where a slave holds SCL LOW to delay communication until it is ready to continue.

16. Which line is used for Clock Stretching?
SCL line.

17. Why is Clock Stretching possible?
Because SCL is an open-drain line. Any device can pull it LOW.

18. Who initiates Clock Stretching?
The slave device.

19. Does clock stretching remove I²C speed limitations?
No. Every I²C device has a maximum supported clock frequency defined in its datasheet. Clock stretching only allows a slave to temporarily delay communication when it needs additional processing time; it does not allow a device to operate beyond its specified I²C speed rating.

20. What is Arbitration?
Arbitration is the process used to decide which master gets control of the I²C bus when two or more masters start communication at the same time.

21. What is Arbitration in I²C?
Arbitration is the mechanism used to determine which master gains control of the I²C bus when multiple masters start transmission simultaneously.

22. Which line is used for Arbitration?
SDA line.

23. Why is Arbitration possible?
Because SDA is open-drain and LOW dominates HIGH.

24. When does a master lose Arbitration?
A master loses arbitration when it transmits a HIGH (1) but reads a LOW (0) on SDA.

25. Clock Synchronization
In multi-master systems: Multiple masters may generate clocks.
Since SCL is open-drain: LOW dominates HIGH
The bus automatically synchronizes to the slower clock.
This ensures reliable communication between masters.

26. Does I²C support multiple masters?
Yes. I²C supports multiple masters connected to the same bus.

27. How is conflict resolved when two masters start simultaneously?
Using arbitration. The master that transmits HIGH but reads LOW loses arbitration and stops transmitting.

28. What is the bus idle condition?
Bus is idle when both SDA and SCL are HIGH.

29. What happens after losing arbitration?
The losing master immediately stops transmission and waits until the bus becomes free again.

30. What is Redundancy?
Redundancy means providing an additional backup device or system so that operation can continue even if the primary device fails.

31. Why is ACK transmitted on the 9th clock pulse?
The first 8 clock pulses are used for one byte of data. The 9th clock pulse is reserved for the receiver to acknowledge successful reception.

32. After sending the address, who generates ACK?
The addressed slave generates ACK.

33. During a write operation, who sends data?
The master sends data and the slave acknowledges it.

34. During a read operation, who sends data?
The slave sends data and the master acknowledges it.

35. How many slaves can be connected to an I²C bus?
With 7-bit addressing, up to 128 addresses exist, of which about 112 are usable because some addresses are reserved. In practice, the number of slaves is limited by bus capacitance and electrical loading rather than the address space.

36. How many masters can be connected to an I²C bus?
The I²C specification does not define a fixed maximum number of masters. Multiple masters can share the bus, and arbitration is used to resolve conflicts when more than one master starts transmission simultaneously. In practice, one or two masters are most common.

37. I²C specification limits bus capacitance to approximately?
400 pF

38. Why does bus capacitance limit the number of I²C devices?
Each device, PCB trace, and cable adds capacitance to SDA and SCL. Since pull-up resistors must charge this capacitance to produce a HIGH level, excessive capacitance causes slow rise times. If the signals do not reach a valid HIGH level before the next clock edge, communication errors can occur. Therefore, bus capacitance limits the practical number of devices and cable length on an I²C bus.

39. Why is Address + R sent again after Repeated START?
The first transaction (Address + W) is used to select the target register inside the slave device. After Repeated START, the master must send Address + R again to inform the slave that the communication direction is changing from write to read. According to the I²C protocol, every START or Repeated START condition must be followed by an address and R/W bit.

40. What is Repeated START?
A Repeated START is a START condition generated without first generating a STOP condition. Notation: Sr

41. What is Repeated START?
A Repeated START is a START condition generated without issuing a STOP condition first. It allows the master to continue communication while retaining control of the bus.

42. Why is Repeated START used?
It is used to change communication direction (write to read or read to write) without releasing the bus.

43. Why is Repeated START preferred over STOP + START?
Because STOP releases the bus. Another master could take control. Repeated START keeps ownership of the bus and allows seamless communication.

44. Why was 10-bit addressing introduced?
To increase the number of available slave addresses from 128 to 1024.

45. How many address bytes are used in 10-bit addressing?
Two address bytes are used.

46. Is 10-bit addressing commonly used?
No. Most I²C devices and embedded systems use 7-bit addressing.

47. Which addressing mode is most common?
7-bit addressing.

48. What is the General Call Address in I²C?
The General Call Address is 0x00. It allows the master to broadcast a write command to all slaves that support General Call.

49. Can General Call be used for reading?
No. General Call supports only write operations because multiple slaves cannot transmit data simultaneously.

50. Do all I²C devices respond to General Call?
No. Support for General Call depends on the device implementation and datasheet specifications

51. What is Clock Synchronization in I²C?
Clock Synchronization is the mechanism used in multi-master I²C systems where multiple masters generate clocks simultaneously. Because SCL is open-drain, the bus automatically synchronizes to the slowest clock.

52. Why does the slower clock dominate?
Because any master can hold SCL LOW, and LOW dominates HIGH on the I²C bus.

53. Is Clock Synchronization required in single-master systems?
No. It is only relevant in multi-master systems.

54. Does I²C support LSB First?
No. I²C always transmits data MSB First.

55. 81. Does I²C require dummy bytes?
No, because clock generation is independent of data transmission.
